# Website Experience Audit — Morris Services / Morris Hauling

**Review date:** 2026-07-10  
**Reviewer stance:** Professional web design agency, pre-public launch critique  
**Scope:** Public experience, careers, customer journey, navigation, visual system, admin/employee *usability polish*  
**Out of scope:** Stripe, CI/CD, env vars, architecture, production readiness scores

**Live site reviewed:** https://www.morris-services.com

---

## Executive verdict

**This is a competent prelaunch brochure with a strong brand mark and honest messaging — not yet a website that wins jobs on first impression.**

It looks like a well-organized product team built a clean Next.js marketing shell around a real operations platform. That shows. What does *not* show is the emotional proof that makes homeowners pick up the phone: trucks in driveways, emptied garages, a face behind the company, a clear “how it works,” and a sense that this crew has done this a hundred times.

**Overall website experience score: 5.8 / 10**

| Audience lens | Score | One-line |
|---------------|------:|----------|
| Homeowner | **5.2** | Clear that you’re not open yet; unclear why I’d wait for *you*. |
| Business / property manager | **4.8** | Almost no commercial story, no account language, no proof. |
| Job applicant | **7.0** | Careers is the richest public experience — still thin on culture. |
| Phone browser | **6.2** | Usable; hero and parent logo work; many pages feel like stacked cards. |

**Would I hire this company based only on the homepage?**  
**No.** I would bookmark it, maybe call if a neighbor recommended you, but the homepage alone does not create hire-ready trust. It creates “interesting local brand in formation.”

# Homepage deep dive (`/`)

### Hero
- **Strength:** Brand-first. The Morris Services logo is the hero — correct for a parent brand. Light background helps logo clarity.
- **Weakness:** No atmospheric photography, no human, no truck, no “place.” It feels like a logo launch page, not a local service empire.
- **Hierarchy issue:** Tagline stack (brand line → “Professional Local Service Companies” → Residential/Commercial line → legal pill) is one too many layers. Visitors get corporate brochure energy, not desire.

### Branding
- Burgundy (`#9B1B30`) + black + light gray is distinctive and more “family holding company” than generic junk-removal orange. Good.
- Logo treatment is the best asset on the parent site.

### Navigation
- Simple umbrella nav (Home, Our Companies, About, Contact) is right-sized.
- Missing: Careers at parent level, Phone in header, maybe “Hauling” as a named nav item.

### Layout / sections
1. Logo hero  
2. Hauling feature card  
3. Five “Coming Soon” future brands  
4. Mission + Contact  

That is a **thin** homepage. After the Hauling card, the future-company grid reads like a wishlist, not a portfolio. Five dashed cards saying the same sentence (“A future Morris Services company…”) dilutes credibility.

### Morris Services vs Morris Hauling positioning
- Structurally correct: parent → operating company → future brands.
- Emotionally incomplete: the parent doesn’t yet feel bigger than Hauling. Hauling is the only real story, so the parent still feels like a wrapper.

### Footer
- Adequate links and mission. Not memorable. No social, no hours, no “call now.”

### Visual hierarchy / spacing / type / images / CTAs
- Spacing is generally generous and modern.
- Typography is clean (product-font feel) — professional, not especially expressive for a local trades brand.
- Images: essentially logo + icons. **No lifestyle or work photography on the parent home.**
- CTAs are polite, not conversion-oriented.

### Would I hire from this homepage alone?
**No.** I’d believe you’re organized. I would not yet believe you’re the crew I want in my garage.

3. **What we take / don’t take**
4. **Pricing explained** (volume, labor, disposal — with 2–3 example scenarios)
5. **Meet the owner** (photo + short story — critical for local trust)
6. **FAQs** (same-day?, stairs?, appliances with freon?, payment, prep tips)
7. **Commercial / property manager** section
8. **Service area map**
9. **Donation / recycling** story (GOT-JUNK-style differentiator)
10. Stronger **Call** CTA sticky on mobile

## What should be removed or demoted
- Dashboard-style “Soon / Preview” stats (or replace with real trust items later: insured, locally owned, response time)
- Duplicate prelaunch paragraphs on every section
- Soft “Visit Website” language on parent when the destination is still thin — or thicken the destination first

## What should be redesigned
- Hauling home below-the-fold: rebuild around customer outcomes, not “system we’re building”
- Services: image-led service pages or at least photo cards
- Pricing: visual volume guide + “example jobs” before raw tiers
- Footer (company): richer, with quick links to process, FAQ, careers, call

# Morris Services parent — five-year brand question

### Does it feel like a future multi-company group?
**Partially, on paper. Not yet in the gut.**

You have the *structure* of a Neighborly-style family of brands (named future companies, parent logo, legal entity). You do **not** yet have the *weight*: case studies, shared standards page, “why Morris Services,” portfolio photography, or a parent story that isn’t “our first company is junk removal.”

### Or a junk company pretending to be a parent?
**Closer to this today** — not because the idea is wrong, but because Hauling is the only company with a banner, phone, services, and careers. The other five brands are identical dashed cards. That pattern is the tell.

### How this should evolve over five years

| Year | Parent site should feel like… |
|------|-------------------------------|
| **Now–1** | “Morris Services presents Morris Hauling” — parent is a short, premium doorway; Hauling is the full experience. Future brands: one tasteful “roadmap” section, not five equal empty cards. |
| **2–3** | Second brand launches with its own hub equal to Hauling. Parent becomes a true portfolio grid with distinct art direction per brand. Shared “Morris Standard” (safety, training, local). |
| **4–5** | Neighborly-lite: parent sells the *family* (trust, careers across brands, commercial multi-service accounts). Each operating company has its own voice; parent is the umbrella of proof. |

**Near-term recommendation:** Stop selling five imaginary companies at equal visual weight. Sell **one real company** under a confident parent brand, with a quiet “more services coming” line.

# Careers audit

### Does it feel like a real company hiring?
**Yes, more than the rest of the marketing site.** Role descriptions, pay ranges, tracks, and “what happens after you apply” are legitimately useful. This is your strongest public narrative.

### Would an applicant trust it?
**Cautiously yes** — if they read the talent-pool labels.  
**Risk:** Listing ~19 roles (ops manager, bookkeeper, mechanic, marketing, etc.) before trucks are rolling can feel like a fantasy org chart. Better: feature 4–6 near-term roles + one general application, and keep the rest behind “future openings” collapsed.

### Should you add…

| Idea | Verdict |
|------|---------|
| Employee photos | **Yes** — even owner + 1–2 early hires. Faces beat icons. |
| Videos | **Yes later** — 60-sec “day on the crew” is gold. |
| Company culture | **Yes** — 4 concrete values with examples, not buzzwords. |
| Growth timeline | You already have a ladder — **keep it**; add real promotion stories when you have them. |
| Benefits graphics | **Yes** when benefits exist; don’t invent. “Training provided / safety gear / room to grow” as simple icons is fine now. |
| Hiring process graphics | You have steps — **elevate visually** (illustrated timeline). |

### What would improve it most
1. Reduce visible role sprawl for prelaunch credibility  
2. Owner photo + “who you’ll work with”  
3. One authentic field photo strip  
4. Clear “actively interviewing” vs “talent pool” visual hierarchy  
5. Mobile sticky Apply CTA on long careers page

**Where confidence increases:** Hauling hero photo · booking preview honesty · careers substance · phone number visibility

**Critical journey fix:** After “Book,” offer a **beautiful early-interest form** (name, phone, zip, junk type, photos) even while booking is closed. Right now the emotional arc peaks at preview and falls off a cliff.
